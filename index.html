§0 The core intuition: what is "energy"?

Before any maths, let's answer a deceptively simple question: how does a machine decide that a photo shows a Beagle rather than a German Shepherd?

The standard answer — the one taught in most ML courses — is: run the image through a neural network, get a vector of numbers called logits, squash them into probabilities with softmax, and pick the highest one. That works, and it works well. But it sidesteps a deeper question: what are those logits actually measuring?

Energy-Based Models give a cleaner answer. Instead of directly predicting probabilities, an EBM asks: how compatible is this image with each possible label? That compatibility score is called the energy. Low energy means "these two belong together." High energy means "this pairing feels wrong."

One-line definition An EBM is a function $E(x, y)$ that takes an input $x$ (an image) and a candidate answer $y$ (a breed label) and returns a single number — the energy. Lower is better. Prediction = find the $y$ with the lowest energy.

Why "energy"? Where does the word come from?

The name is borrowed from physics. In nature, systems settle into their lowest-energy state: a ball rolls to the bottom of a bowl, water flows downhill, an electron drops to the lowest available orbital. The key insight is that stable, likely states have low energy and unstable, unlikely states have high energy.

ML borrows this exact intuition. We want the model to assign low energy to correct (image, label) pairs — those are the "stable" configurations — and high energy to incorrect ones. Training is the process of shaping this energy landscape so that it has deep valleys exactly where the correct answers live.

Physical analogy — the valley landscape
Picture a hilly terrain where the horizontal axes represent all possible (image, label) pairs and the altitude represents energy. A ball dropped anywhere on this terrain will roll downhill and settle in a valley. Those valleys are where the model's answers live.

Training carves the terrain: it digs valleys under correct pairs and builds hills under incorrect ones. At test time, you drop a new image onto the map and ask — which label's valley does it roll into?

The model doesn't say "73% Beagle." It says "Beagle scores −8.3, everything else scores much higher, so the answer is Beagle." The gap between energies is what matters, not any single value. This is a fundamentally different — and in many ways more honest — way to express confidence.

Important The energy values themselves have no fixed units or scale. −8.3 doesn't mean anything in isolation. What matters is the relative ordering: Beagle has lower energy than all other breeds for this image, so it wins. LeCun et al. (2006) call this "uncalibrated" energy — and it's a feature, not a bug. You're free to design the energy function however you like, without any normalisation constraint.

How is this different from a standard classifier?

In a standard neural network classifier, the model is trained to directly output a probability distribution over classes. To do that, it must normalise: every output must be between 0 and 1, and they must all sum to 1. This normalisation constraint (the softmax + cross-entropy pipeline) is so deeply baked in that most practitioners never think about it.

But normalisation is expensive. To say "the probability of class A is 73%," you implicitly need to evaluate and compare all other classes simultaneously. For 37 pet breeds that's fine. For language models with a vocabulary of 50,000 words, it's manageable but nontrivial. For generative models where the "answer space" is every possible image — it's utterly intractable.

EBMs sidestep this entirely. You don't need to normalise. You just need the minimum — which answer has the lowest energy? That's a fundamentally easier computation, and it opens up architectures and training strategies that probabilistic models simply cannot use.

Standard classifier

Input → network → logits → softmax → probabilities

Must normalise over all classes at every step. Probabilities must sum to 1.

Energy-based model

Input + candidate answer → network → energy scalar

No normalisation required. Just find the answer with the lowest energy.

What does training actually do?

Training an EBM has one job: shape the energy landscape so that correct (input, answer) pairs always sit in valleys, and incorrect ones sit on hills. LeCun et al. (2006) describe this as a two-force process happening at every training step:

Force 1
Push down ↓
Decrease the energy of the correct (image, label) pair — dig the valley deeper.
→
Force 2
Pull up ↑
Increase the energy of incorrect pairs — especially the ones that are dangerously close to the valley floor.
→
Result
Energy gap
A clear margin separates correct answers (deep valleys) from incorrect ones (high hills).

Both forces are necessary. Without force 1, the model never learns what's correct. Without force 2, the model can cheat: it can make everything low-energy, earning zero loss while being completely useless. This cheating strategy is called collapse, and it's the central failure mode in EBM training — choosing the right loss function is precisely the art of preventing it. We'll come back to this in §4.

Both forces are visible in the training loop. In engine.py, after every epoch the evaluator tracks exactly these two quantities separately:

# engine.py — evaluate()
correct_e  = energies[torch.arange(batch_size), targets]       # Force 1: correct-class energy
incorrect_e = energies[mask].view(batch_size, -1).mean(dim=-1) # Force 2: mean incorrect energy

# Reported metrics:
"mean_correct_energy":   ...,   # should decrease each epoch  ↓
"mean_incorrect_energy": ...,   # should increase each epoch  ↑
"mean_energy_gap":       ...,   # the margin between the two  →

If training is working, mean_correct_energy trends negative, mean_incorrect_energy trends positive, and the gap widens. At convergence our model reaches a mean correct energy of −8.3 and a mean gap of ~8.5 — exactly the numbers you'll see in §10.

Three reasons to care about EBMs

1. Architectural freedom. Any function that outputs a scalar can be an energy function — neural nets, kernel methods, handcrafted scoring functions. There's no invertibility requirement (unlike normalising flows), no decoder constraint (unlike VAEs), no output-distribution assumption (unlike most generative models). In practice this means you can swap architectures freely. Our transformer.py ships three interchangeable energy backbones — tiny (256-d, 4 layers), small (384-d, 6 layers), and base (768-d, 12 layers) — all sharing the same EBM loss and inference code unchanged.

2. Natural out-of-distribution detection. A standard softmax classifier is forced to assign high probability to some class even for completely alien inputs — it sums to 1 by construction, so it can't say "I don't know." An EBM can: if an input doesn't look like anything in training, all energies will be high. That universal high energy is a clean signal for "this is outside my knowledge." Our engine.py already surfaces mean_energy_gap as a diagnostic — a small gap means the model is uncertain, a large one means it's confident. §10 shows how to use this directly for OOD detection on the Oxford Pets test set.

3. Composability. Because energies are just numbers, you can add energy functions from multiple models: $E_\text{total}(x, y) = E_\text{colour}(x, y) + E_\text{shape}(x, y)$. Combining probabilistic models requires multiplying distributions and renormalising — which can be intractable. Adding scalars is always trivial.

Where we're headed The rest of this post builds EBMs from the ground up: the energy function (§1), turning energy into probability via the Gibbs distribution (§2), the partition function and why it's both important and painful (§3), how to train without collapse (§4), and finally a Vision Transformer that uses all of this to classify 37 pet breeds at 90.8% accuracy (§5–§10).

§1 Building block 1: the energy function

§0 told us what energy means. This section answers the practical question: what exactly is the energy function, and how do you build one?

Why does the answer $y$ go inside the function?

This is the question most introductions skip, and it's the most important one. In a standard classifier you feed in an image $x$ and get out probabilities — the label $y$ never goes in. So why does an EBM take both $x$ and $y$ as inputs?

Because an EBM is asking a fundamentally different question. A standard classifier asks: "given this image, what is the label?" An EBM asks: "given this image and this candidate label, how well do they fit together?" The answer is the energy. You run this question separately for every possible label, then pick whichever label produced the lowest energy.

Analogy — the compatibility test
Think of a job interview. The interviewer (the model) evaluates each candidate (a label $y$) against the job description (the image $x$) and assigns a fit score. The best-fit candidate gets hired — they have the lowest "mismatch energy." The key point: the score only makes sense as a pair. You can't score a candidate without knowing the job, and you can't score a job without knowing the candidate.

This pairing is what makes EBMs general. It works when $y$ is a single class label (classification), a sequence of words (language modelling), a segmentation mask (computer vision), or even a continuous image (generative modelling). The form of the function changes; the idea does not.

The formal definition

Definition (Energy function)
An energy function is a parameterised scalar-valued function of two inputs: $$E_\theta : \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{R}$$ Read this as: "$E_\theta$ takes a point from the input space $\mathcal{X}$ (e.g. all possible images) and a point from the output space $\mathcal{Y}$ (e.g. all class labels) and returns a single real number." The subscript $\theta$ means the function has learnable parameters — weights that get updated during training. Lower energy = the pair $(x, y)$ fits together well. Higher energy = poor fit.

What makes a valid energy function?

Almost anything. That is not an exaggeration — it is the defining feature of EBMs and what separates them from every probabilistic model. The only requirement is that $E_\theta(x, y)$ outputs a real number. There is no normalisation constraint, no invertibility requirement, no assumption about the form of a distribution. LeCun et al. (2006) put it directly: "One advantage of the energy-based approach is that it puts very little restriction on the nature of $E$."

To appreciate why this matters, contrast it with models that do have architectural constraints:

Normalising flow

Must be invertible. Every layer must have a computable inverse. Rules out most modern architectures.

VAE decoder

Must output valid probability parameters (e.g. means and variances of a Gaussian). Form of output is prescribed.

EBM energy function

Any function that outputs a scalar. Neural net, kernel, dot product, L2 distance — or anything you design for your problem.

Two flavours: unconditional and conditional

Depending on the task, we use one of two forms:

Unconditional — $E_\theta(x)$

Scores a single data point $x$ on its own. Used in generative modelling, where we want to learn which images are natural (low energy) vs. which are noise (high energy). The model asks: "does this image look like real data?"

Conditional — $E_\theta(x, y)$

Scores an (input, answer) pair. Used in classification and structured prediction, where we want to find which $y$ fits a given $x$ best. The model asks: "does this label fit this image?" — which is exactly what we want for pet breed classification.

For the rest of this post we use the conditional form exclusively. Every time you see $E_\theta(x, y)$, read it as "the incompatibility score of image $x$ paired with breed label $y$."

The energy function we actually use

For classification with $C$ classes, the most natural and widely used energy function is:

$$E_\theta(x,\, y{=}c) \;=\; -\,h_\theta(x)^\top w_c \;-\; b_c$$

There are three ingredients. Let's unpack each one carefully before combining them.

$h_\theta(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ — the image representation. The neural network (in our case, a Vision Transformer) processes the raw image $x$ and produces a vector of $d$ numbers. Think of this as the network's internal "summary" of the image — a point in a high-dimensional space where similar images end up near each other. Our EBT uses $d = 384$, so every image becomes a point in 384-dimensional space. In transformer.py this is the CLS token, extracted on the first line after the Transformer blocks:

# transformer.py — EnergyBasedTransformer.forward()
x = self.patch_embed(x)   # image → patch sequence
x = self.blocks(x)         # 6 Transformer layers
h = self.norm(x[:, 0])     # x[:,0] is the [CLS] token → h_θ(x) ∈ ℝ^384

$w_c \in \mathbb{R}^d$ — the class template vector. For each of the $C$ classes, there is a learned vector of the same dimension $d$. It represents the "ideal direction" in that 384-dimensional space for class $c$ — the direction that Beagles tend to cluster around, or German Shepherds, or Persian Cats. These are learned from data during training. In code, all 37 class templates and their biases live inside a single linear layer:

# transformer.py — EnergyBasedTransformer.__init__()
self.energy_head = nn.Linear(embed_dim, num_classes)
# weight shape: [37, 384]  — row c is w_c
# bias shape:  [37]        — element c is b_c

$h_\theta(x)^\top w_c$ — the dot product (alignment score). The dot product between two vectors measures how much they point in the same direction. Geometrically:

$$h^\top w_c = \|h\|\,\|w_c\|\,\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors. When the image representation $h$ and the class template $w_c$ point in nearly the same direction ($\theta \approx 0°$), $\cos\theta \approx 1$ and the dot product is large and positive — the image looks like it belongs to class $c$. When they point in opposite directions ($\theta \approx 180°$), the dot product is large and negative — the image looks nothing like class $c$.

Geometric picture — vectors in representation space
Imagine a compass. The class template $w_c$ is a fixed arrow pointing in the "Beagle direction." The image representation $h_\theta(x)$ is another arrow, pointing wherever the Transformer thinks this image lives. If a Beagle photo comes in, $h$ points roughly in the same direction as $w_\text{Beagle}$ — large dot product. If a Persian Cat comes in, $h$ points somewhere else entirely — small or negative dot product. The energy is just the negation of this alignment score, so high alignment = low energy = confident match.

The negative sign. We negate the dot product because of the EBM convention: lower energy means better fit. High alignment (large dot product) should mean low energy. Negating flips this: $E = -h^\top w_c - b_c$. Large positive dot product → large negative energy → correct answer gets a deep valley. This single negation is the entire EBM-specific change to a standard classifier, and it happens in exactly one line:

# transformer.py — EnergyBasedTransformer.forward()
logits = self.energy_head(h)   # shape [batch, 37] — these are the raw dot products + bias
return -logits                  # negate → energies. high alignment = negative energy = valley

Step 1 — alignment. Compute $s_c = h_\theta(x)^\top w_c + b_c$ for each class $c$. This is the "logit" — the raw score before any normalisation. High $s_c$ means the image representation aligns well with class $c$'s template direction.

Step 2 — negate to get energy. Define $E_\theta(x, c) = -s_c = -h_\theta(x)^\top w_c - b_c$. Now high alignment → negative energy → sits in a valley.

Step 3 — inference. For a new image $x$, compute $E_\theta(x, c)$ for all $C$ classes and pick the winner: $$\hat{y} = \arg\min_c \; E_\theta(x, c) = \arg\max_c \; \bigl(h_\theta(x)^\top w_c + b_c\bigr)$$ This is identical to a softmax classifier finding the maximum logit. The EBM framing and the standard classifier framing are mathematically the same thing — just described differently. We will make this connection explicit in §2.

Where does $h_\theta(x)$ come from?

So far we've treated $h_\theta(x)$ as a black box — "the neural network produces a vector." But the choice of architecture matters enormously, because the quality of the representation determines how well the dot-product energy can separate classes. A poor representation puts Beagles and Basset Hounds in nearby regions of space; a rich one separates them cleanly.

This is where Vision Transformers come in. A ViT processes the image as a sequence of 16×16 pixel patches, runs them through layers of self-attention, and produces a special output vector called the [CLS] token — a single 384-dimensional summary of the entire image. That CLS vector is $h_\theta(x)$. We feed it directly into the dot-product energy formula above.

The full pipeline in one line $$\underbrace{x}_{\text{image}} \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{ViT}\;}\; \underbrace{h_\theta(x)}_{\text{CLS vector, } \mathbb{R}^{384}} \;\xrightarrow{\;-(\cdot)^\top w_c - b_c\;}\; \underbrace{E_\theta(x,c)}_{\text{energy scalar}} \;\xrightarrow{\;\arg\min_c\;}\; \underbrace{\hat{y}}_{\text{predicted breed}}$$ We'll build every piece of this pipeline in §5–§8. For now, hold the formula — and the geometric intuition behind it — as the definition we are implementing.

§2 Building block 2: turning energy into probability

At the end of §1 we could already make predictions: compute the energy for every class and pick the minimum. For many tasks, that's all you need. But there are situations where you want more than just the winning answer — you want to know how confident the model is, or you want to combine this model's output with another model's, or you want a loss function that can pull up on all incorrect answers simultaneously rather than just the worst one. For all of this, you need probabilities, not just a ranking.

The question is: given an energy function that outputs arbitrary real numbers, how do you convert those numbers into a valid probability distribution? This is what the Gibbs distribution does.

Why $\exp(-E)$ and not something simpler?

Before writing the formula, it's worth asking why the exponential is the right choice. Three properties make it uniquely suited:

1. It is always positive. Probabilities must be non-negative. Raw energies can be anything — including negative (recall Beagle's energy was −8.3). Applying $\exp(-E)$ maps any real number to a strictly positive score: $\exp(-{-8.3}) = \exp(8.3) \approx 4000$, and $\exp(-{+2.7}) = \exp(-2.7) \approx 0.07$. Both are positive.

2. Ratios depend only on differences. The ratio of probabilities for two configurations $A$ and $B$ is: $$\frac{p(A)}{p(B)} = \frac{\exp(-E(A))}{\exp(-E(B))} = \exp\!\bigl(E(B) - E(A)\bigr)$$ The normalising constant $Z$ cancels completely. This means probability ratios are entirely determined by energy differences, not absolute values. It doesn't matter whether energies are large or small in absolute terms — only the gap between them matters. This is the mathematical justification for LeCun's observation that individual energy values are "uncalibrated."

3. Small energy differences produce large probability differences. Because of the exponential, a gap of just 4 energy units produces a probability ratio of $e^4 \approx 55\times$. The distribution is sharp — it concentrates probability mass strongly at the minimum, which is exactly what we want for confident classification.

The Gibbs distribution for classification

For classification with $C$ discrete classes, we want the conditional probability $p(y{=}c \mid x)$ — the probability that the correct label is class $c$ given input $x$. The Gibbs distribution gives:

Definition (Conditional Gibbs distribution)
$$p_\theta(y = c \mid x) \;=\; \frac{\exp(-E_\theta(x,\, c))}{\displaystyle\sum_{c'=1}^{C} \exp(-E_\theta(x,\, c'))}$$ The numerator scores class $c$ specifically. The denominator — the partition function $Z(x) = \sum_{c'} \exp(-E_\theta(x, c'))$ — sums over all $C$ classes and forces the probabilities to sum to 1.

Read the formula in plain English: the probability of class $c$ is proportional to how low its energy is, relative to the energies of all other classes. A class with energy much lower than the rest monopolises almost all the probability mass.

Substituting the §1 energy function

We don't need to treat this formula abstractly — we can substitute our specific energy from §1 right now. Recall: $E_\theta(x, c) = -h_\theta(x)^\top w_c - b_c$. Plugging in:

$$\exp(-E_\theta(x, c)) \;=\; \exp\!\bigl(h_\theta(x)^\top w_c + b_c\bigr)$$

The double negation removes the minus sign. Now substitute into the full formula:

$$p_\theta(y = c \mid x) \;=\; \frac{\exp\!\bigl(h_\theta(x)^\top w_c + b_c\bigr)}{\displaystyle\sum_{c'=1}^{C} \exp\!\bigl(h_\theta(x)^\top w_{c'} + b_{c'}\bigr)}$$
The central insight of this post Let $z_c = h_\theta(x)^\top w_c + b_c$ be the logit for class $c$. Then: $$p_\theta(y = c \mid x) \;=\; \frac{e^{z_c}}{\sum_{c'} e^{z_{c'}}} \;=\; \text{softmax}(z)_c$$ This is exactly the softmax classifier — the standard output layer used in virtually every neural network classification pipeline. The logit $z_c$ is simply the negative energy: $z_c = -E_\theta(x, c)$. Every softmax classifier you have ever trained is an energy-based model. You were doing EBM inference the whole time without knowing it.

This equivalence has three concrete consequences worth pausing on:

You already know how to implement this. The pipeline is unchanged: network → linear layer → softmax. The EBM framing doesn't add computation — it adds interpretation. The "logits" you compute are negative energies. The softmax denominator is the partition function $Z(x)$. The cross-entropy loss you already use is the negative log-likelihood loss — which we'll see in §4 is exactly the "good" EBM loss function.

The distinction is interpretive, and interpretation pays off. Once you see your classifier as an EBM, you gain tools that the probabilistic view doesn't naturally offer: out-of-distribution detection via minimum energy, model composition via energy addition, and the freedom to swap in non-probabilistic loss functions (hinge loss, margin loss) that are sometimes more efficient.

The equivalence breaks in harder settings. For classification with 37 classes, $Z(x)$ is a sum of 37 terms — trivially computed. The equivalence holds perfectly. But for generative models where $y$ ranges over all possible images, $Z$ becomes a high-dimensional integral with no closed form. There the EBM and probabilistic views diverge sharply, and the EBM approach offers a genuine escape route. §3 explores exactly this.

A note on temperature

The full Gibbs distribution includes an inverse temperature parameter $\beta > 0$:

$$p_\theta(y = c \mid x) = \frac{\exp(-\beta \, E_\theta(x,c))}{\sum_{c'} \exp(-\beta \, E_\theta(x,c'))}$$

When $\beta$ is large (low temperature), the distribution becomes very sharp — almost all probability mass concentrates at the minimum-energy class. When $\beta$ is small (high temperature), the distribution flattens and becomes more uniform. For classification we typically set $\beta = 1$ and let the model learn appropriately scaled energies. But $\beta$ becomes important in generative settings — diffusion models, for instance, anneal $\beta$ during sampling, starting hot (uniform) and cooling toward the data distribution.

What §3 covers next The denominator $Z(x) = \sum_{c'} \exp(-E_\theta(x,c'))$ looks innocuous here — just 37 additions. But in other settings it becomes a sum (or integral) over an astronomically large or even infinite space. That is the partition function problem, and it is the central computational challenge of energy-based modelling. §3 examines when it is tractable, when it is not, and what researchers do about it.

§3 Building block 3: the partition function $Z$

In §2 we saw that converting energy to probability requires a normalising denominator — a sum of $\exp(-E)$ over all possible answers. That denominator is the partition function, written $Z$. It is the central computational obstacle of energy-based modelling: easy to define, trivial in some settings, and completely intractable in others.

What $Z$ actually is

For our conditional classifier, the partition function is not a global constant — it is a different number for every input image. For image $x$, it sums over the answer space $\mathcal{Y}$:

Definition (Partition function — conditional form)
$$Z(x) \;=\; \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} \exp\!\bigl(-E_\theta(x,\, y)\bigr)$$ For our 37-class classifier, $\mathcal{Y} = \{1, \ldots, 37\}$ and this is a sum of exactly 37 terms. The Gibbs probability from §2 is then: $$p_\theta(y = c \mid x) \;=\; \frac{\exp(-E_\theta(x,c))}{Z(x)}$$ $Z(x)$ is the total "score budget" for image $x$. Each class takes a share proportional to its Boltzmann factor $\exp(-E)$.

Notice $Z$ depends on $x$, not just $\theta$. Every image in a batch has its own partition function value. This is what §2's worked example computed: $Z(\text{Beagle image}) \approx 4024$, with the Beagle class contributing 4023 of that and the remaining 36 classes sharing the rest.

The tractability spectrum

Whether computing $Z(x)$ is easy or hard depends entirely on the size and structure of the answer space $\mathcal{Y}$. LeCun et al. (2006) categorise this precisely, identifying five levels of increasing difficulty — from trivially exact to genuinely intractable. For our purposes, the key insight is that $Z$ is only a sum (or integral) over the answer space $\mathcal{Y}$. The difficulty comes from how large and continuous $\mathcal{Y}$ is, not from the input $x$.

Our model — trivial

$\mathcal{Y} = \{1,\ldots,37\}$

$$Z(x) = \sum_{c=1}^{37} e^{-E(x,c)}$$ 37 exponentiations, 1 sum. Exact every forward pass.

Language model — manageable

$\mathcal{Y} = $ vocabulary, $|\mathcal{Y}| \approx 50{,}000$

$$Z(x) = \sum_{w \in \mathcal{V}} e^{-E(x,w)}$$ 50k terms. Still a finite sum — expensive but exact.

Image generation — intractable

$\mathcal{Y} = \mathbb{R}^{224 \times 224 \times 3}$

$$Z(x) = \int_{\mathcal{Y}} e^{-E(x,y)}\, dy$$ Continuous, infinite-dimensional. $256^{150528} \approx 10^{362{,}000}$ discrete states. No closed form.

All three columns sum over the same thing — the answer space $\mathcal{Y}$. Only its cardinality and structure change. This is the point the standard presentation obscures by jumping straight to the intractable case.

Why $Z$ is only half the problem

Computing $Z(x)$ in a forward pass is only the first issue. The deeper problem arises during training, when we need the gradient of $\log Z(x)$ with respect to the parameters $\theta$.

Taking the derivative of $\log Z(x) = \log \sum_y \exp(-E_\theta(x,y))$ with respect to $\theta$ (LeCun et al., 2006, Section 2.2):

$$\frac{\partial \log Z(x)}{\partial \theta} \;=\; -\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} \frac{\partial E_\theta(x,y)}{\partial \theta} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\exp(-E_\theta(x,y))}{Z(x)}}_{=\;p_\theta(y \mid x)}$$ $$= \;-\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} p_\theta(y \mid x)\;\frac{\partial E_\theta(x,y)}{\partial \theta} \;=\; -\,\mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}\!\left[\frac{\partial E_\theta(x,y)}{\partial \theta}\right]$$

The gradient of $\log Z$ is an expectation of the energy gradient under the model's own current distribution $p_\theta(y \mid x)$. To evaluate it exactly, you need to compute $p_\theta(y \mid x)$ for every possible $y$ — which requires evaluating $Z(x)$ in the first place. This is the circularity at the heart of EBM training.

The real problem is not Z — it is sampling from $p_\theta$ For 37 classes: $p_\theta(c \mid x)$ is computed exactly via softmax — 37 terms, done. The expectation $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E]$ is a weighted sum of 37 gradient vectors. Exact, cheap, no approximation needed.

For a generative model where $y$ is a 224×224 image: drawing a sample from $p_\theta(y \mid x)$ requires sampling from a distribution over $10^{362{,}000}$ states. There is no closed form. Every approximation strategy for intractable EBMs is an answer to this one question: how do you approximate $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E]$ without enumerating $\mathcal{Y}$?

The full NLL loss gradient — push down and pull up

Putting it together: the gradient of the NLL loss $\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+) + \log Z(x)$ is:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\partial E_\theta(x, y^+)}{\partial \theta}}_{\text{push correct energy down}} \;-\; \underbrace{\sum_{c=1}^{C} p_\theta(c \mid x)\;\frac{\partial E_\theta(x,c)}{\partial \theta}}_{\text{pull all incorrect energies up, weighted by } p_\theta}$$

The first term pushes the correct class energy down. The second term — the gradient of $\log Z$ — is a probability-weighted pull-up on every class simultaneously. Classes the model currently finds most likely get pulled up hardest. This is the "dig valleys, build hills" principle from §0, expressed in exact mathematics.

When the model is perfectly trained, $p_\theta(y^+ \mid x) = 1$ for all training samples. This means $p_\theta(c \mid x) = 0$ for all $c \neq y^+$. The pull-up term becomes:

$$\sum_{c} p_\theta(c \mid x)\,\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x,c) = 1 \cdot \nabla_\theta E_\theta(x, y^+) + 0 + \ldots = \nabla_\theta E_\theta(x, y^+)$$

The push-down and pull-up terms become equal and opposite. The gradient is zero — the model has stopped updating because the landscape is already correctly shaped. $\blacksquare$

Approximation strategies when $Z$ is intractable

For generative EBMs where $\mathcal{Y}$ is too large to enumerate, every training algorithm is an approximation of the gradient $\nabla_\theta \log Z(x)$. LeCun et al. (2006) analyse these as different strategies for choosing which incorrect answers to pull up. Three canonical approaches:

Contrastive Divergence (Hinton, 2002). Instead of summing over all $y \in \mathcal{Y}$, draw a single approximate sample $\tilde{y}$ by running a short Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) chain starting from the correct answer $y^+$. Use $\tilde{y}$ as the sole "negative" to pull up. This approximates $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E]$ with a single sample near the data manifold — cheap, but biased.

Score Matching (Hyvärinen, 2005). Sidestep $Z$ entirely by observing that the gradient of $\log p_\theta$ with respect to $y$ — called the score — does not depend on $Z$: $\nabla_y \log p_\theta(y) = -\nabla_y E_\theta(y)$. Train the model to match the score of the data distribution instead of maximising likelihood. No sampling required; the cost is computing second derivatives of $E$.

Noise Contrastive Estimation (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010). Replace the intractable sum over $\mathcal{Y}$ with a binary classification problem: train a classifier to distinguish real data samples from samples drawn from a known noise distribution $q(y)$. The optimal classifier implicitly recovers $\log p_\theta / q$ — and therefore the energy — without ever computing $Z$.

For our EBT — none of this is needed $|\mathcal{Y}| = 37$. The expectation $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E]$ is a weighted sum of 37 terms, computed exactly as the softmax-weighted average of 37 gradient vectors. The full NLL loss gradient is evaluated in closed form at every training step — no MCMC, no score matching, no noise distribution. This is what LeCun et al. (2006) call the "trivial" case: the partition function does not depend on $W$ in a way that makes gradient computation hard. Classification with a small discrete label set is the cleanest possible instantiation of EBM training. §4 builds on exactly this.

§4 Building block 4: training — sculpting the landscape

We now have an energy function (§1), a way to turn it into probabilities (§2), and a precise understanding of the normalising constant that makes that conversion possible (§3). The remaining question is: how do we adjust the parameters $\theta$ so that the energy landscape has the right shape?

Start with what "right shape" looks like visually.

At epoch 1 the surface is almost perfectly flat. The model assigns roughly equal energy to this class for every possible image — it has no opinion yet. The "well depth" of 1.8 shown in the corner is the gap between the minimum energy on the surface and the surrounding plateau. A depth of 1.8 means the model is only slightly more confident about Abyssinian features than noise. Correspondingly, the blue test samples sit in a shallow dip but are not meaningfully separated from the surrounding terrain.

By epoch 50 a deep, tight basin has formed precisely where Abyssinian representations cluster in PCA space. The well depth has grown to ~8.4 — matching the mean energy gap in our final results (§10). Test samples sit at the bottom. Everything else is high energy. Training is the process of digging that basin.

What makes a loss function good or bad?

We need a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ whose minimisation causes the energy landscape to develop exactly this structure. But not every loss function achieves this — and this is a point LeCun et al. (2006) make forcefully in Section 5 of their tutorial.

The core requirement is that minimising $\mathcal{L}$ must simultaneously do two things: push the energy of the correct answer down, and push the energies of incorrect answers up. A loss that only does the first — pushes down — will lead to collapse: the model learns to make everything low energy, earning zero loss while being completely useless. The flat surface at epoch 1 is almost exactly this state.

LeCun et al. define a formal condition (Condition 3, Section 5.2) that a loss must satisfy to guarantee a correct energy ordering. Checking common losses against this condition:

Energy loss — bad

$$\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+)$$

Only pushes correct energy down. No pull-up term at all. For flexible architectures, the model collapses: sets all energies to zero and satisfies the loss trivially. Flat surface forever.

Perceptron loss — marginal

$$\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+) - \min_y E_\theta(x, y)$$

Pulls up the model's current best answer. Zero margin — no guaranteed energy gap between correct and incorrect. Valley may form, but nothing enforces its depth.

NLL loss — good

$$\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+) + \log Z(x)$$

Pulls up all incorrect answers simultaneously, weighted by how likely the model currently thinks they are. Margin $> 0$ guaranteed. The basin deepens every step.

The NLL loss — term by term

From §2 and §3 we have both pieces of the NLL loss. Putting them together:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; \underbrace{E_\theta(x,\, y^+)}_{\text{Term 1}} \;+\; \underbrace{\log\sum_{c=1}^{C} \exp\!\bigl(-E_\theta(x,\,c)\bigr)}_{\text{Term 2} = \log Z(x)}$$

Term 1 is the energy of the correct class $y^+$. Every gradient step decreases it — the blue basin in the GIF gets pushed lower. This is the push-down force.

Term 2 is $\log Z(x)$ — the log-partition function derived in §3. Its gradient is the probability-weighted pull-up on all class energies. Concretely:

$$\frac{\partial\,\log Z(x)}{\partial\theta} \;=\; -\sum_{c=1}^{C} p_\theta(c \mid x)\;\frac{\partial E_\theta(x,c)}{\partial\theta}$$

Each class is pulled up with force proportional to $p_\theta(c \mid x)$ — the model's current probability estimate for that class. The classes the model is most confused about get the hardest pull-up. This is why the surrounding plateau in the GIF rises as the basin deepens: the gradient isn't just digging the correct valley, it is simultaneously raising everything else.

The cross-entropy equivalence — and what it buys The NLL loss above is mathematically identical to $\texttt{F.cross\_entropy}(\texttt{logits}{=}{-}\texttt{energies},\;\texttt{targets})$. You have been training EBMs every time you used cross-entropy — you just didn't have the interpretive frame. The EBM view buys you three things standard cross-entropy does not label for you: the well depth as a calibrated confidence metric, the minimum energy as an OOD score, and the freedom to swap in non-probabilistic losses (hinge, margin) when the application demands it.

What the well depth measures

The "well depth" printed in the GIF corner is the energy gap:

$$\text{well depth} \;=\; E_\theta(x,\, c_2) \;-\; E_\theta(x,\, c_1) \qquad c_1 = \arg\min_c E,\quad c_2 = \arg\min_{c \neq c_1} E$$

It is the difference between the lowest-energy class and the second-lowest. A depth of 1.8 at epoch 1 means the model can barely rank the correct class first. A depth of 8.4 at epoch 50 means the correct class has a valley 8.4 energy units deeper than the closest competitor — translating to a probability of $\approx 99.98\%$ for the top class (as computed in §2).

This metric is tracked throughout training as mean_energy_gap. In §10 you will see it plotted against training epoch — watching it grow from ~1.8 to ~8.4 is watching the landscape sculpture happen in numbers. The GIF above is the same process shown spatially.

Suppose we train with only Term 1 — the energy loss $\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+)$. The gradient $\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x, y^+)$ pushes the correct class energy down toward $-\infty$. Nothing prevents any other class from also having low energy. A model with enough capacity will find the path of least resistance: set $E_\theta(x, c) \approx -K$ for all $c$ and all $x$, for some large constant $K$. The loss becomes $-K$, arbitrarily negative, with zero gradient. But the landscape is perfectly flat — the model predicts every class with equal probability.

This is why LeCun et al. (2006) call the energy loss "bad" for general architectures. The two-column panel in this section's Fig. 11 of the GIF shows this: the flat red surface at epoch 1 is almost exactly this collapsed state. Only Term 2 — the log-partition pull-up — prevents it from staying there. $\blacksquare$

What training does not tell you

One thing §4 deliberately does not cover is which neural network computes $E_\theta(x, c)$. The loss is architecture-agnostic: any function that maps (image, class) to a scalar and is differentiable can be trained with this loss. The choice of architecture determines the quality of the energy landscape — how well-separated the valleys are, how smoothly the hills rise, how transferable the features are.

Choosing that architecture is the subject of §5 and §6. The short answer is: a Vision Transformer is a particularly good choice, and there is a deep theoretical reason why.

§5 The backbone: Vision Transformer (ViT)

We have the full EBM recipe now — energy function, Gibbs distribution, partition function, loss. But all of it rests on one thing we haven't chosen yet: what neural network actually computes the energy? Pick a bad one and the Beagle valley is shallow and the German Shepherd valley is right next to it. Pick a good one and the 37 valleys are deep, tight, and well-separated. Everything in §§1–4 is only as good as the representations underneath it.

We use a Vision Transformer (ViT). Not just because it produces strong features — CNNs do too — but because its core mechanism, self-attention, turns out to be performing a form of energy minimisation inside every layer. The model isn't just a feature extractor with an EBM bolted on top. It is energy-based all the way through. §6 will make that precise. First, the architecture itself.

Step 1 — patchification and projection

Transformers process sequences of tokens. A raw image is not a sequence, so the first step converts it into one. The 224×224×3 input is divided into a grid of non-overlapping $16{\times}16$ patches — the coloured squares in the left panel of Fig. 5:

$$N = \frac{224}{16} \times \frac{224}{16} = 14 \times 14 = 196 \text{ patches}$$

Each patch contains $16 \times 16 \times 3 = 768$ pixel values. These are linearly projected to a $d$-dimensional embedding vector, giving one token per patch:

$$\mathbf{e}_i = W_E\,\text{vec}(\text{patch}_i) + \mathbf{b}_E \qquad W_E \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times 768}, \quad i = 1,\ldots,196$$

For our EBT-small, $d = 384$. In practice this projection is implemented as a strided convolution — nn.Conv2d(3, 384, kernel_size=16, stride=16) — which is mathematically equivalent to the matrix multiply above but more memory-efficient. The pretrained ViT-B/16 variant uses $d = 768$.

Step 2 — CLS token and positional embeddings

Two elements are added before the sequence enters the encoder. First, a learnable [CLS] token $\mathbf{x}_\text{CLS} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is prepended — the orange square in Fig. 5. It starts as a random vector with no image content, but through self-attention it learns to query all 196 patch tokens and aggregate their information into a single vector. At the output, this vector becomes $h_\theta(x)$ — the image representation that the energy head dot-products against class templates. The CLS token is the interface between the Transformer's internal processing and the EBM energy function.

Second, learnable positional embeddings $\mathbf{E}_\text{pos} \in \mathbb{R}^{(N+1) \times d}$ are added element-wise. Without them, self-attention is permutation-invariant — the model cannot distinguish a patch from the top-left corner from one at the bottom-right. Positional embeddings inject spatial structure:

$$\mathbf{z}_0 = \bigl[\mathbf{x}_\text{CLS} \;;\; \mathbf{e}_1 \;;\; \ldots \;;\; \mathbf{e}_N\bigr] + \mathbf{E}_\text{pos} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{197 \times d}$$

This sequence of 197 tokens — one CLS plus 196 patch tokens, each $d$-dimensional — is what enters the encoder.

Step 3 — transformer encoder blocks

Each encoder block (the "Transformer Encoder × N" box in Fig. 5) applies two sub-layers with residual connections, using pre-norm ordering — LayerNorm is applied before each sub-layer, not after:

$$\mathbf{z}' = \mathbf{z} + \text{MHSA}\!\bigl(\text{LN}(\mathbf{z})\bigr)$$ $$\mathbf{z}'' = \mathbf{z}' + \text{FFN}\!\bigl(\text{LN}(\mathbf{z}')\bigr)$$

Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA). Every one of the 197 tokens attends to every other. Using $H$ parallel attention heads, each with key/query dimension $d_k = d / H$, attention head $i$ computes:

$$\text{head}_i = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q_i K_i^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V_i \qquad Q_i = \mathbf{z}W_{Q}^{(i)},\; K_i = \mathbf{z}W_{K}^{(i)},\; V_i = \mathbf{z}W_{V}^{(i)}$$

The $H$ heads are concatenated and projected: $\text{MHSA}(\mathbf{z}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1,\ldots,\text{head}_H)\,W_O$. For EBT-small, $H = 6$ heads and $d_k = 384/6 = 64$.

Feed-Forward Network (FFN). A two-layer MLP with GELU activation and expansion ratio 4, applied independently to each token:

$$\text{FFN}(\mathbf{z}) = \text{GELU}(\mathbf{z}W_1 + \mathbf{b}_1)\,W_2 + \mathbf{b}_2 \qquad W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times 4d},\; W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{4d \times d}$$

Dropout ($p = 0.1$) is applied inside both sub-layers during training for regularisation. For EBT-small, each block has $d=384$, $H=6$, and inner FFN dimension $4 \times 384 = 1536$.

Step 4 — extracting the image representation

After $L$ encoder blocks, the CLS token has accumulated information from every patch through repeated rounds of attention. A final LayerNorm is applied before extraction:

$$h_\theta(x) \;=\; \text{LN}\!\bigl(\mathbf{z}_L^{[0]}\bigr) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^d$$

where $\mathbf{z}_L^{[0]}$ denotes position 0 (the CLS slot) of the final layer's output. This is $h_\theta(x)$ — the same vector that appeared in every energy formula since §1. Its quality determines everything: well-separated breed representations produce deep, narrow energy valleys; entangled representations produce shallow, overlapping ones.

§6 The Hopfield connection: attention is energy minimisation

§5 ended with a question: why is a Transformer a natural EBM backbone at a deeper level than "it produces good features"? A CNN also produces good features — but a CNN computes them using local receptive fields, building up representations hierarchically with no explicit notion of compatibility or energy. The CLS vector it would produce is just a feature vector. Good, but inert.

A Vision Transformer is different. It computes its internal representations by solving a small energy minimisation problem at every layer. The CLS token that emerges at the end is not just a summary — it is the result of iterative energy-based retrieval across every patch in the image. When our energy head then computes $-h_\theta(x)^\top w_c$, it is placing one more energy minimisation on top of a stack of them. The whole model is a nested EBM — internal retrieval feeding into external classification energy. This section shows exactly why.

Start here: what is a Hopfield Network?

A Hopfield Network is one of the oldest ideas in neural networks — proposed by John Hopfield in 1982. The concept is simple: store a set of memories (patterns), then given a noisy or partial query, retrieve the closest stored memory. The retrieval process is defined as energy minimisation — the query state rolls downhill on an energy surface until it settles into the valley of the nearest stored pattern.

Classic Hopfield Networks had limited capacity and only worked reliably for a small number of binary patterns. In 2020, Ramsauer et al. showed that a modern continuous version — with a different energy function — has exponential storage capacity, and more strikingly, that its retrieval update rule is exactly the softmax attention used in Transformers. Attention was already Hopfield retrieval. Nobody had named it that way.

The modern Hopfield energy

Suppose we have $M$ stored patterns — think of them as "memories" — arranged as rows of a matrix $K \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times d}$. We receive a query vector $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and want to retrieve the most compatible stored pattern. The energy that governs retrieval is:

$$E(\mathbf{q}) \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{\beta}\log\sum_{i=1}^{M} \exp\!\bigl(\beta\, \mathbf{q}^\top K_i\bigr) \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{q}\|^2 \;+\; \text{const}$$

Take this apart term by term.

Term 1: $-\frac{1}{\beta}\log\sum_i \exp(\beta\,\mathbf{q}^\top K_i)$. This is the negative log-sum-exp of the dot products between $\mathbf{q}$ and every stored pattern. Recall from §3 that log-sum-exp is a smooth approximation to the maximum — it is large when at least one dot product $\mathbf{q}^\top K_i$ is large. Negating it means this term is low when $\mathbf{q}$ aligns strongly with at least one stored pattern. This is exactly the EBM compatibility structure from §1: low energy = good match. The parameter $\beta$ controls sharpness — high $\beta$ makes it a hard maximum (retrieve only the best pattern); low $\beta$ blends contributions from multiple patterns.

Term 2: $\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{q}\|^2$. A regularisation term that prevents $\mathbf{q}$ from drifting to infinity along the direction of the best-matching pattern. Without it, the energy has no lower bound and minimisation never converges. Together, the two terms create a well-defined energy landscape with stable minima near the stored patterns.

Connection to §1's energy function Our classification energy was $E_\theta(x, c) = -h_\theta(x)^\top w_c - b_c$ — a negative dot product between the image representation and the class template. The Hopfield energy's first term, $-\frac{1}{\beta}\log\sum_i \exp(\beta\,\mathbf{q}^\top K_i)$, is a soft version of the same idea: negative alignment between a query and a set of stored directions. Both measure compatibility through dot products. The difference is that Hopfield uses a smooth maximum over $M$ patterns; our classifier uses the exact negative dot product for each class separately.

Proof: minimising the energy gives attention

What does it mean to "retrieve" a pattern? It means finding the $\mathbf{q}$ that minimises $E(\mathbf{q})$. We do this by taking the gradient with respect to $\mathbf{q}$ and finding its zero. The result — which we state now and prove below — is the fixed-point equation:

$$\mathbf{q}^* \;=\; K^\top\,\text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{K\mathbf{q}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)$$

This is exactly scaled dot-product attention applied to a single query. The full proof is four steps and uses only the chain rule and the log-sum-exp identity from §3.

Proof — Hopfield fixed-point is scaled dot-product attention

Step 1 — differentiate Term 1. Let $s_i = \beta\,\mathbf{q}^\top K_i$ be the scaled dot product with pattern $i$. The first term of the energy is $-\frac{1}{\beta}\log\sum_i e^{s_i}$. Differentiating with respect to $\mathbf{q}$, and using the chain rule $\partial s_i / \partial \mathbf{q} = \beta K_i$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{q}}\left(-\frac{1}{\beta}\log\sum_i e^{s_i}\right) \;=\; -\frac{1}{\beta} \cdot \frac{\sum_i \beta K_i\, e^{s_i}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}} \;=\; -\sum_i K_i \cdot \underbrace{\frac{e^{s_i}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}}}_{ =\;\text{softmax}(\beta K\mathbf{q})_i}$$

The derivative of the log-sum-exp is the softmax of the arguments — the same identity that appeared in §3 when deriving $\nabla_\theta \log Z$. So Term 1's gradient is $-K^\top\,\text{softmax}(\beta K\mathbf{q})$, a softmax-weighted sum of the stored patterns.

Step 2 — differentiate Term 2. $\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{q}}\,\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{q}\|^2 = \mathbf{q}$. Straightforward.

Step 3 — set the full gradient to zero. Summing both terms:

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial \mathbf{q}} \;=\; -K^\top\,\text{softmax}(\beta K\mathbf{q}) \;+\; \mathbf{q} \;=\; \mathbf{0}$$ $$\Longrightarrow\qquad \mathbf{q}^* \;=\; K^\top\,\text{softmax}(\beta K\mathbf{q})$$

This is the fixed-point equation: the retrieved state equals the softmax-weighted sum of stored patterns, where the weights measure how well each pattern aligns with the current query. Run iteratively until convergence — or run once for a single-step approximation.

Step 4 — substitute Transformer notation. Set $\beta = 1/\sqrt{d_k}$, replace the single query vector $\mathbf{q}$ with a matrix of queries $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_k}$ (one row per token), and separate keys $K$ from values $V$ (the Transformer's generalisation that decouples what to match against from what to retrieve):

$$\mathbf{q}_t^* = K^\top\,\text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{K\mathbf{q}_t}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{all } T \text{ queries simultaneously}\;}\; \text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

One step of Hopfield energy minimisation = one head of scaled dot-product attention. $\blacksquare$

Two things are worth pausing on after this proof.

First, the $\beta = 1/\sqrt{d_k}$ choice is not arbitrary scaling. In the Hopfield framework $\beta$ is inverse temperature — it controls how sharply the softmax concentrates on the best-matching pattern. At high $d_k$, dot products $\mathbf{q}^\top K_i$ grow in magnitude proportional to $\sqrt{d_k}$, pushing softmax toward saturation (near-zero gradients). Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ keeps the softmax inputs in a stable range regardless of dimension — this is the Transformer's temperature schedule built in by design, not a heuristic.

Second, the proof uses a single gradient step as the retrieval rule rather than running to true convergence. Ramsauer et al. (2020) show that for well-separated stored patterns, a single step already retrieves the correct pattern with exponentially high probability. In practice the Transformer stacks $L$ blocks — each block is one step of this iteration — and convergence emerges from depth rather than iteration within a single block.

The Hopfield–Attention equivalence (Ramsauer et al., 2020)
One step of modern Hopfield energy minimisation = one head of scaled dot-product attention, with $\beta = 1/\sqrt{d_k}$.

Consequence 1: attention scores $QK^\top/\sqrt{d_k}$ are negative energies — a high attention score means low energy means high compatibility between that query token and that key token.

Consequence 2: every Transformer block runs $H$ parallel energy minimisation steps — one per attention head — each retrieving from a different learned subspace of the representation.

Consequence 3: the softmax in attention is the Gibbs distribution from §2, applied to the internal token-level energies rather than the output class-level energies.

What this means for the EBT specifically

Our EBT-small runs 6 encoder blocks, each with 6 attention heads. That means 36 parallel energy minimisation steps happen between the raw patch embeddings and the CLS token that feeds the energy head. At each step, the model asks: for this token, which other tokens are most compatible? It routes information from the most compatible tokens and suppresses the rest.

The CLS token is particularly important here. Unlike patch tokens, which only attend to spatially nearby regions in early layers, the CLS token attends globally from the first block. It progressively accumulates the results of 6 rounds of energy-based retrieval across all 196 patch tokens. By the time it exits the final LayerNorm, it encodes a representation shaped by repeated compatibility-driven routing — not by fixed local filters.

When the energy head computes $E(x, c) = -h_\theta(x)^\top w_c$, it adds one more energy minimisation on top: which class direction $w_c$ is most compatible with this fully retrieved CLS vector? The classification answer is the outcome of a 37-way energy comparison sitting on top of 36 internal ones.

The nested EBM structure
$$\underbrace{E_\text{internal}^{(1)}, \ldots, E_\text{internal}^{(36)}}_{\text{attention heads: token-level retrieval}} \;\longrightarrow\; h_\theta(x) \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{E(x,c) = -h_\theta(x)^\top w_c}_{\text{energy head: class-level retrieval}}$$ The model does not compute features and then measure energy. It measures energy continuously, at every layer, all the way to the output.

This is also why the attention maps the model produces at each layer have an interpretable meaning beyond "what the model looks at": each attention weight is a normalised compatibility score — a Gibbs probability over tokens — produced by one step of an internal energy minimisation. In §10 you will see these maps visualised directly, showing the model attending to breed-discriminative regions (face shape, ear structure, coat texture) layer by layer, long before the energy head ever runs. We will also walk through the code that extracts them.

§7 Putting it together: the Energy-Based Transformer

The preceding six sections established, in sequence, what an energy function is, how it induces a probability distribution via the Gibbs transform, why the partition function is trivially tractable for classification, which loss criterion correctly sculpts the landscape, why a Vision Transformer is the natural architecture for producing the representation $h_\theta(x)$, and why that claim has a precise theoretical grounding in the Hopfield–attention equivalence. What remains is the question of how these abstractions compose into a single, trainable object. The answer reduces to four lines:

x = self.patch_embed(x)         # (B, 197, 384)
x = self.blocks(x)              # (B, 197, 384) — 36 Hopfield steps
h = self.norm(x[:, 0])          # (B, 384)      — h_θ(x), the CLS vector
energies = -self.energy_head(h) # (B, 37)       — E(x,c) = -(Wh+b)_c

Every architectural and theoretical commitment made in §§1–6 is present in these lines. The remainder of this section examines each in turn, making the correspondence between theory and implementation explicit.

The patch embedding and positional structure

The patch embedding module realises the patchification and linear projection described in §5. Rather than implementing the projection matrix $W_E \in \mathbb{R}^{384 \times 768}$ explicitly, the module uses a strided convolution with kernel size equal to patch size — a standard equivalence that avoids materialising the flattened patch vectors:

class PatchEmbedding(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, img_size=224, patch_size=16,
                 in_channels=3, embed_dim=384):
        self.num_patches = (img_size // patch_size) ** 2      # 196
        self.proj = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, embed_dim,
                              kernel_size=patch_size, stride=patch_size)
        self.cls_token = nn.Parameter(torch.randn(1, 1, embed_dim) * 0.02)
        self.pos_embed = nn.Parameter(
            torch.randn(1, self.num_patches + 1, embed_dim) * 0.02
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        B = x.shape[0]
        x   = self.proj(x).flatten(2).transpose(1, 2)  # (B, 196, 384)
        cls = self.cls_token.expand(B, -1, -1)          # (B,   1, 384)
        x   = torch.cat([cls, x], dim=1)                # (B, 197, 384)
        return x + self.pos_embed

The output is the sequence $\mathbf{z}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{197 \times 384}$ from §5 — 196 patch tokens with spatial content, prepended by a CLS token initialised from a zero-mean Gaussian with standard deviation 0.02, all shifted by the learned positional embedding matrix. The CLS token at this stage carries no image information; its content is entirely determined by the subsequent attention layers, which is precisely the mechanism §5 identified as the interface between the Transformer's internal processing and the energy head.

The encoder blocks and their energy-theoretic interpretation

Each of the six encoder blocks implements the pre-norm variant of the standard Transformer block — LayerNorm applied before each sub-layer rather than after, which confers better gradient flow for deep networks. By the Hopfield equivalence of §6, the multi-head attention sub-layer is simultaneously performing six parallel energy minimisation steps, each in a 64-dimensional projected subspace ($d_k = 384 / 6 = 64$):

class TransformerBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, embed_dim=384, num_heads=6,
                 mlp_ratio=4.0, drop=0.1):
        self.norm1 = nn.LayerNorm(embed_dim)
        self.attn  = nn.MultiheadAttention(
            embed_dim, num_heads, dropout=drop, batch_first=True
        )
        self.norm2 = nn.LayerNorm(embed_dim)
        self.mlp   = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(embed_dim, int(embed_dim * mlp_ratio)),  # 384 → 1536
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Dropout(drop),
            nn.Linear(int(embed_dim * mlp_ratio), embed_dim),  # 1536 → 384
            nn.Dropout(drop),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        x_norm = self.norm1(x)
        attn_out, _ = self.attn(x_norm, x_norm, x_norm)
        x = x + attn_out
        x = x + self.mlp(self.norm2(x))
        return x

The call self.attn(x_norm, x_norm, x_norm) — with identical query, key, and value arguments — is self-attention in its standard form. Crucially, the attention weight matrix computed internally, $\text{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_k})$, is precisely the Gibbs distribution from §2 applied at the token level: each row is a probability distribution over the 197 tokens, peaked at the tokens most compatible with the current query under the Hopfield energy of §6. The FFN sub-layer, operating independently on each token position, provides the non-linear transformation necessary for the representations to separate in the space where the energy head subsequently operates. Six such blocks, each contributing six attention heads, yield 36 energy minimisation steps before $h_\theta(x)$ is computed.

The energy head and the reduction to §1

Following the final encoder block, the CLS token — at position index 0 of the sequence — is extracted and passed through a LayerNorm, yielding $h_\theta(x) = \text{LN}(\mathbf{z}_L^{[0]}) \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$. This vector is then mapped to $C = 37$ scalar energies by a single linear layer:

class EnergyBasedTransformer(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, img_size=224, patch_size=16, in_channels=3,
                 embed_dim=384, depth=6, num_heads=6,
                 mlp_ratio=4.0, num_classes=37, drop=0.1):
        self.patch_embed = PatchEmbedding(img_size, patch_size,
                                           in_channels, embed_dim)
        self.blocks      = nn.Sequential(*[
            TransformerBlock(embed_dim, num_heads, mlp_ratio, drop)
            for _ in range(depth)
        ])
        self.norm        = nn.LayerNorm(embed_dim)
        self.energy_head = nn.Linear(embed_dim, num_classes)

    def forward(self, x):
        x        = self.patch_embed(x)
        x        = self.blocks(x)
        h        = self.norm(x[:, 0])
        energies = -self.energy_head(h)   # E(x,c) = -(Wh+b)_c
        return energies

The negation in -self.energy_head(h) warrants attention. PyTorch's nn.Linear computes the affine map $Wh + b$, producing the vector of logits familiar from standard classification. Negating this vector yields $E(x,c) = -(Wh + b)_c = -h_\theta(x)^\top w_c - b_c$ — precisely the energy function defined in §1. The negation is the sole syntactic distinction between an EBM and a conventional softmax classifier at the implementation level, confirming the equivalence established analytically in §2. What the EBM framing adds is not computational machinery but interpretive structure: the logits are negative energies, the softmax denominator is the partition function $Z(x)$, and the cross-entropy loss is the NLL loss of §3 and §4.

§8 Dataset, training, and evaluation

Oxford-IIIT Pets

The Oxford-IIIT Pets dataset comprises 37 fine-grained pet breed categories — 12 cat breeds and 25 dog breeds — with approximately 200 images per class. The standard splits are 3,680 images for trainval and 3,669 for test. What makes the dataset non-trivial for a classification model is not scale but granularity: many breed distinctions rest on subtle morphological features — ear shape, facial proportions, coat texture — rather than coarse categorical differences. A model that assigns low energy to the correct breed must have learnt representations sensitive enough to separate, for instance, an Abyssinian from an Egyptian Mau, or a Beagle from a Basset Hound. The loss function

The NLL loss derived in §3 and §4 — $\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+) + \log Z(x)$ — is implemented in loss.py. The implementation uses torch.logsumexp rather than torch.log(torch.sum(torch.exp(...))) for the partition function term; the two are mathematically equivalent but logsumexp subtracts the maximum before exponentiating, preventing numerical overflow when energies are large in magnitude: 

def energy_loss(energies, targets):
    """
    NLL loss under the Gibbs distribution.
    L = E(x, y+)  +  log Z(x)
    Term 1 pushes the correct-class energy down.
    Term 2 (= log-partition) pulls all energies up,
    weighted by their current softmax probability.
    """
    batch_size = energies.shape[0]
    # Term 1: energy of the correct class for each sample
    correct_energy = energies[
        torch.arange(batch_size, device=energies.device), targets
    ]
    # Term 2: log Z(x) = log Σ_c exp(-E(x,c))  — numerically stable
    log_partition = torch.logsumexp(-energies, dim=-1)

    return (correct_energy + log_partition).mean()

The indexing expression energies[torch.arange(batch_size, device=energies.device), targets] selects, for each sample in the batch, the energy assigned to its ground-truth class. The device=energies.device argument ensures the index tensor is allocated on the same device as energies — omitting it causes a silent device mismatch error when training on GPU.

Training procedure

Training follows a standard deep learning recipe with two choices tuned specifically for the EBT. AdamW is used as the optimiser at an initial learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$, with weight decay providing implicit $L_2$ regularisation on the Transformer weights. The learning rate is annealed via cosine schedule over the full training run, decaying to a floor of $10^{-6}$. Gradient clipping at max-norm 1.0 stabilises training in early epochs when the energy landscape is still nearly flat and gradients can spike.

In the codebase, the per-epoch loop is encapsulated in train_one_epoch in engine.py, which handles gradient accumulation, logging at 20-batch intervals, and returns the mean epoch loss. The scheduler advances at each optimiser step — not each epoch — so the cosine decay is applied at the batch level:

optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=3e-4)
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(
    optimizer, T_max=total_steps, eta_min=1e-6   # total_steps = epochs × batches
)

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for images, labels in train_loader:
        energies = model(images)                        # (B, 37)
        loss     = energy_loss(energies, labels)        # NLL under Gibbs

        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
        optimizer.step()
        scheduler.step()                                # per-step, not per-epoch

Evaluation metrics

Standard classification accuracy — fraction of test images where $\arg\min_c E_\theta(x,c)$ equals the ground-truth label — is the primary metric, but the EBM framing makes several additional quantities meaningful. After each training epoch, evaluate() in engine.py computes five metrics on the test split:

Accuracy

Fraction of samples where the minimum-energy class matches the ground truth. Final value: 90.8%.

Mean correct energy

Average of $E_\theta(x, y^+)$ over the test set — the depth of the correct-class valley. Final value: $\approx -8.3$.

Mean incorrect energy

Average energy of the incorrect classes — the height of the surrounding hills. Final value: $\approx +0.2$.

Mean energy gap

$E_\theta(x, c_2) - E_\theta(x, c_1)$ — the gap between the two lowest energies, measuring basin depth. Tracks the well-depth evolution seen in §4. Final value: $\approx 8.5$.

NLL loss

The evaluation loss under the Gibbs distribution, serving as the primary scalar training signal logged to history.json.

Together, the energy metrics tell a richer story than accuracy alone. A model could achieve 90% accuracy with shallow, overlapping valleys — marginally correct but fragile. The mean energy gap of ~8.5 confirms that the basins are deep and well-separated: the correct class is not merely the minimum, it is the minimum by a wide margin, corresponding to a Gibbs probability of approximately 99.98% as computed in §2. These metrics are plotted in §10.

§9 Inference: navigating the energy landscape

A trained EBT-small achieves 90.8% top-1 accuracy on the Oxford Pets test split, with a mean energy gap of ~8.5 between the predicted class and the nearest competitor. What does inference look like operationally, and what does the energy framing give you beyond a standard argmax over logits?

The prediction rule is straightforward: for a test image $x$, run the forward pass to obtain the 37-dimensional energy vector, then return the minimum-energy class:

$$\hat{y} \;=\; \arg\min_{c \in \{1,\ldots,37\}} E_\theta(x, c) \;=\; \arg\max_{c} \,p_\theta(c \mid x)$$

The two formulations are equivalent by the monotonicity of $\exp$, as established in §2. The energy view, however, exposes two quantities that the probability view obscures: the absolute level of the energies across all classes, and the gap between the minimum and its nearest competitor. Both carry information that a softmax output discards by construction.

Out-of-distribution detection via free energy

A softmax classifier must distribute probability mass over its $C$ output classes — regardless of whether the input belongs to any of them. Given an image of a car, a model trained on pet breeds is forced to assign high probability to some breed, because the denominator is fixed. The energy view has no such constraint.

For an in-distribution image, at least one class will have strongly negative energy — the CLS representation aligns well with its class template. The Boltzmann factor $\exp(-E(x, c))$ for that class will be large, and the log-partition function $\log Z(x) = \log\sum_c \exp(-E(x,c))$ will be dominated by that term. For an out-of-distribution image, no class template aligns well with the representation: all energies are moderately positive, all Boltzmann factors are small, and $\log Z(x)$ is correspondingly low.

This motivates the free energy score proposed by Liu et al. (2020) as the OOD detection criterion:

$$\mathcal{F}(x) \;=\; -\log Z(x) \;=\; -\log\sum_{c=1}^{C}\exp\!\bigl(-E_\theta(x,c)\bigr) \;=\; -\text{LogSumExp}_c\!\bigl(-E_\theta(x,c)\bigr)$$

An in-distribution image has a low free energy $\mathcal{F}(x)$ (large $\log Z$); an OOD image has a high free energy (small $\log Z$). A threshold $\tau$ on $\mathcal{F}(x)$ separates the two. In code, it is more natural to work with $-\mathcal{F}(x) = \log Z(x)$ as a positive-direction score — high values indicate in-distribution:

Common confusion $-\mathcal{F}(x) = \text{LogSumExp}_c(-E_\theta(x,c))$ is not equal to $-\min_c E_\theta(x,c) = \max_c(-E_\theta(x,c))$. The log-sum-exp is a smooth upper bound on the maximum — it accounts for contributions from all classes, not just the most compatible one. Liu et al. (2020) show empirically that the free-energy score outperforms the max-logit and max-softmax-probability baselines on standard OOD benchmarks, precisely because it aggregates evidence across the full class set rather than reducing to a single maximum.

Energy gap as calibrated confidence

The energy gap between the top-two predictions quantifies how unambiguously the minimum-energy class was selected:

$$\Delta E(x) \;=\; E_\theta(x,\, c_2) \;-\; E_\theta(x,\, c_1) \qquad c_1 = \arg\min_c E,\quad c_2 = \arg\min_{c \neq c_1} E$$

Because $\Delta E$ enters the Gibbs distribution exponentially, it translates directly into a probability ratio:

$$\frac{p_\theta(c_1 \mid x)}{p_\theta(c_2 \mid x)} \;=\; \frac{\exp(-E(x,c_1))}{\exp(-E(x,c_2))} \;=\; \exp\!\bigl(\Delta E(x)\bigr)$$

A gap of $\Delta E = 1.0$ corresponds to a 2.7× probability ratio — the model marginally prefers $c_1$. A gap of $\Delta E = 8.4$ — the mean at convergence — corresponds to a ratio of $e^{8.4} \approx 4{,}400\times$, or equivalently a Gibbs probability of ~99.98% for the top class (as computed in §2). The gap is therefore a calibrated, interpretable confidence measure that does not require post-hoc temperature scaling or Platt calibration.

This is in contrast to softmax probabilities, which are known to be poorly calibrated for overparameterised models — a softmax output of 99% does not reliably correspond to 99% empirical accuracy. The energy gap makes no probabilistic claim beyond the ratio above, and that ratio is exact given $\beta = 1$.

Inference implementation

In the codebase, all four inference quantities — prediction, probabilities, free-energy score, and energy gap — are computed inside evaluate() in engine.py as part of the evaluation loop over the full test dataloader. The computation for a single batch is:

# energies: (B, 37) — output of model(images)

# 1. Prediction: minimum-energy class
preds = energies.argmin(dim=-1)                         # (B,)

# 2. Gibbs probabilities (β=1)
probs = torch.softmax(-energies, dim=-1)                # (B, 37)

# 3. Free-energy score: high → in-distribution, low → OOD
#    -F(x) = log Z(x) = LogSumExp_c(-E(x,c))
in_dist_score = torch.logsumexp(-energies, dim=-1)      # (B,)

# 4. Energy gap: ΔE = E(c₂) - E(c₁), ascending sort
sorted_e = energies.sort(dim=-1).values                 # ascending: index 0 = min
confidence_gap = sorted_e[:, 1] - sorted_e[:, 0]       # (B,)  always ≥ 0

Note that torch.logsumexp(-energies, dim=-1) is $\log Z(x)$, not $-\log Z(x)$. It is used as a positive OOD score: the higher its value, the more in-distribution the input. Thresholding this score below some $\tau$ flags a sample as OOD. The threshold itself is calibrated on a held-out validation set — engine.py does not apply a threshold; the raw scores are returned for downstream analysis.

Temperature at inference time

Throughout this post, $\beta = 1$ has been assumed in all Gibbs computations. At inference time, $\beta$ can be tuned post-hoc to adjust the sharpness of the output distribution without retraining: increasing $\beta$ sharpens the distribution toward a hard $\arg\min$; decreasing $\beta$ flattens it toward uniform. As $\beta \to \infty$, the Gibbs distribution collapses to a point mass at the minimum-energy class. For the energy gap and free-energy score, $\beta$ scales both quantities linearly — a gap of 8.4 at $\beta = 1$ becomes a gap of 16.8 at $\beta = 2$, with the probability ratio growing as $e^{16.8} \approx 2 \times 10^7$. In practice, the model is well-calibrated at $\beta = 1$ and no post-hoc adjustment is applied in our experiments.

What the plot does confirm is that the CLS representations have learnt a semantically meaningful neighbourhood structure: breeds that look similar are placed near each other in representation space, which is a necessary but not sufficient condition for the energy landscape to have the geometry observed in §10a and §10e.

10h. Energy gap as calibration signal

§9 derived that the energy gap $\Delta E(x) = E(c_2) - E(c_1)$ translates exactly to a probability ratio $\exp(\Delta E)$ under the Gibbs distribution. If the model is well-calibrated, this ratio should track empirical accuracy: samples where $\Delta E$ is large should be classified correctly more often than samples where $\Delta E$ is small. Fig. 11 tests this directly.

Key findings — Energy gap calibration

The bar chart in Fig. 11 (left) confirms the theoretical prediction from §9: accuracy increases monotonically with energy gap across all ten decile bins without exception. The lowest-gap bin (mean gap ≈ 0.7) achieves only ~58% accuracy — the model is nearly guessing when the two lowest-energy classes are nearly tied. The highest-gap bin (mean gap ≈ 7.8) achieves ~100% accuracy — when the correct class valley is deep and unambiguous, the model is almost never wrong.

The reliability curve (right) provides finer resolution. The rise from 50% at gap ≈ 0 to near-100% is roughly sigmoidal, with the steepest gain between gaps 0 and 3. Beyond gap 4, the curve is essentially flat at 99–100%. This means that a single threshold of approximately $\Delta E \approx 3$ separates ambiguous predictions (below: ~80% accurate) from confident predictions (above: ~99% accurate) — a usable abstention criterion requiring no post-hoc fitting.

Key takeaways

1. Every softmax classifier is an EBM. The standard neural network classification pipeline — features → linear layer → softmax → cross-entropy — is mathematically identical to an energy-based model with conditional Gibbs distribution and negative log-likelihood training. The EBM framework doesn't change what you compute; it changes how you think about what you compute.

2. The energy interpretation gives you more. Once you view your model through the energy lens, you get natural tools for OOD detection (high energy = unfamiliar), confidence estimation (energy gap between top classes), and model composition (sum energies from multiple models).

3. The hard part is when Z is intractable. For classification, the partition function is just a sum over classes — trivially computed. The real challenges of EBMs emerge in generative settings where $Z$ must be approximated. This is where contrastive divergence, score matching, and Langevin dynamics become essential.

4. Vision Transformers are natural EBM backbones. The global self-attention mechanism lets every patch attend to every other, building rich representations. The [CLS] token provides a natural aggregation point. The architecture's flexibility — no invertibility constraints, no generative decoder — is exactly what EBMs need.

What comes next?

Joint energy models (JEM): Use the same energy function for both classification and generation — $p(x) \propto \sum_y \exp(-E(x,y))$ — training with MCMC-based generation.

Diffusion models as EBMs: Score-based diffusion models learn $\nabla_x \log p(x)$ — the gradient of the energy surface — and use it for generation via Langevin dynamics.

Compositional generation: Because energies are additive scalars, you can compose multiple EBMs: $E_{\text{total}}(x) = E_{\text{color}}(x) + E_{\text{shape}}(x)$ to generate images satisfying multiple constraints simultaneously.
